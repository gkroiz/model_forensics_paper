\textcolor{red}{[A. But what is the motivation framing] Determining whether a model is misaligned is a major goal of safety research. Prior work aims to identify misalignment behaviorally, but the same behavior can stem from entirely different motivations -- some concerning, others benign. This raises the problem of \textit{model incrimination}: given a concerning action, is it actually due to malign intent (incrimination), or does it have a more benign explanation (exoneration)?}

\textcolor{blue}{[B. Misbehavior is not misalignment framing] Determining whether a model is misaligned is a major goal of safety research. Prior work has largely focused on detecting concerning behavior, but behavior alone is not sufficient to establish misalignment: a concerning action can arise from prosaic causes such as confusion. This raises the problem of \textit{model incrimination}: given a concerning action, is it actually due to malign intent (incrimination), or does it have a more benign explanation (exoneration)?}

\textcolor{teal}{[C. Justifying a serious response framing] Prior work in AI safety research has focused on detecting model misbehavior. But justifying a serious response requires more than a concerning action: we need to determine what motivates the misbehavior. We call this problem \textit{model incrimination}: given a concerning action, is it actually due to malign intent (incrimination), or does it have a more benign explanation (exoneration)?}

\noindent\textbf{[Impact]}

[BABA]Determining whether a model is misaligned is a major goal of safety research. Prior work has largely focused on detecting concerning behavior, but behavior alone is not sufficient to establish misalignment: a concerning action can arise from prosaic causes such as confusion. This raises the problem of \textit{model incrimination}: given a concerning action, is it actually due to malign intent (incrimination), or does it have a more benign explanation (exoneration)? To study this problem, we construct a diverse suite of N environments where models exhibit ostensibly concerning behaviors, and then investigate the motivations behind the behaviors. We follow a three-step protocol -- reading CoTs to generate hypotheses for what motivated an action, environment counterfactuals to test those hypotheses, and triangulating across independent experiments to verify whether findings converge -- and find that model motivations can often be concretely determined. For example, we find that Kimi K2 Thinking disabling type checking settings rather than fixing type errors reflects genuine laziness, and that DeepSeek R1's sandbagging of a math evaluation stems from confusion over user intent. However, some questions remain unresolved, such as whether Kimi K2 Thinking believes it is going against the user's wishes while disabling the type checking settings. Overall, our investigations demonstrate that model incrimination is a difficult but tractable empirical problem, and we open source our environments at \url{https://github.com/gkroiz/agent-interp-envs} to encourage future work.
